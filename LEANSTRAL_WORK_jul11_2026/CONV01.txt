⏺ Good instinct to filter for meaning. The trap with a theorem prover is picking
  something that feels hard but is hollow. Let me name the two hollow traps
  first, then give you demos that aren't.

  What is NOT meaningful (avoid)

  - A "famous hard theorem." Either it's already in mathlib (so the proof exists
  — you'd be re-deriving, which is circular and any Lean user shrugs at), or
  it's genuinely open (a 6B-active local model won't do novel research math).
  Both flop.
  - Anything needing the paper's giant budgets. Their AVL demo was 2.7M tokens /
  22 compactions over hours. In LMStudio 4-bit you'll be slower and
  budget-limited. Pick something that lands in tens of thousands of tokens, few
  turns. Difficulty of the statement isn't the wow — the wow is that the Lean 
  kernel independently certifies the result, so zero trust in the LLM is 
  required. Lead with that.

  The meaningful demo: catch a real bug, then prove the fix

  This is the one I'd run. It's the paper's own strongest result and it's
  non-circular, has stakes, and lands with non-mathematicians:

  1. Take a small real function with a subtle boundary bug — integer overflow,
  off-by-one, saturating arithmetic. (The paper's own example: zigzag/varint
  decode overflowing at U64.MAX on value+1. Great candidate — you can hand-model
  it in Lean without the Aeneas pipeline.)
  2. Have Leanstral state the correctness property and try to prove it → it
  fails / proves the negation → concrete counterexample = bug found.
  3. Fix the code.
  4. Leanstral proves the property → machine-checked guarantee.

  That full arc ("the machine found the bug and certified the repair") is the
  meaningful story. Good candidate functions: binary search (bounds +
  returns-index-iff-present), saturating/wrapping arithmetic, a varint
  encode/decode round-trip, GCD correctness/termination.

  Strong alternative: the anti-hallucination contrast

  Dramatizes why formal methods matter to an AI-skeptical audience. Give a
  subtly false statement to a normal chatbot → it produces a convincing bogus
  "proof." Give it to Leanstral → it cannot prove it (because it's false) and
  instead proves the negation. Punchline: a Lean proof physically cannot lie;
  the kernel checks it. Cheap to run, rhetorically brutal.

  The personal-hook version

  You do LLM benchmarking. Formally verify a property of your own eval code —
  e.g., "this sampler draws without replacement," "this score aggregation is
  order-independent / bounded." "We machine-proved our own benchmark harness is
  correct" is meaningful precisely because your colleagues rely on that code.

⏺ It's the right question — and it's exactly the question a sharp colleague will
  fire back after reading the email, so we need a real answer, not "it's just a
  demo." Here's the honest one.

  Yes, an LLM will usually catch B and A by eye. I'd concede that up front. So
  the value isn't the catching. It's three things an LLM fundamentally cannot
  do:

  1. Certificate vs. guess. "I think this is right" is a probability; a Lean
  proof is checked by a tiny trusted kernel. Your E-prover folks already live
  this distinction — a heuristic prover's claim isn't the same object as a
  checked derivation. Same code that wrote the bug is the code you'd ask "is it
  fixed?" — it shares the coder's blind spot. The kernel doesn't.
  2. ∀ vs. sampling. The hero step isn't finding the bug — it's the fix. An LLM
  (and fuzzing, and unit tests) says "looks right on the cases I considered."
  Lean says proven for all inputs, including the boundary you didn't imagine.
  That's a categorical difference over an infinite input space, and it's
  precisely where coordinate/round-trip bugs hide.
  3. Can't hallucinate a proof. Ask an LLM to "prove" a subtly false property
  and it'll produce a confident, wrong argument. A prover just fails — so you
  learn the truth instead of a plausible story.

  And the part that makes it intellectually honest: B and A are eyeballable on 
  purpose. They're small enough that your audience can independently verify the
  tool isn't cheating — you can't do that with a 200-line proof. The real payoff
  is that the same machinery scales to code no human or LLM can check by
  inspection (the paper's AVL O(log n) proof — 2.7M tokens — is the extreme). So
  the demo's arc is: "here's a bug you could've caught by eye — but the output
  is a certificate, and the same pipeline certifies things no one can eyeball."

  The cleanest way to show the gap rather than assert it: add a short beat where
  we hand a plain LLM (or Leanstral without the verifier) a subtly-false
  variant of the spec — it "proves" it convincingly — then Lean refuses. That
  dramatizes points 1 and 3 in ~60 seconds and preempts the skeptic.

  One decision: do we include that anti-hallucination contrast beat, or keep the
  demo to the clean B→A find-and-fix and just address the skepticism in the
  email prose? My lean: include it — it's the difference between telling your
  colleagues formal methods matter and showing them.
